Popular music now accounts for 52% of the entries on Korea's weekly performance box office chart, up two percentage points from the previous week, as a cluster of arena-scale idol tours opened in Busan and Incheon. Of the 50 productions ranked by the Korea Performing Arts Box Office Information System (KOPIS) for the 28th week of 2026, 26 were pop music events — the single largest genre block, and enough to push the top-genre concentration ratio from 50% to 52%.

## The Tours That Moved the Needle

Thirty productions entered the chart for the first time this week, an unusually heavy intake that reflects how concert touring works: a run opens, sells its inventory in a compressed window, and exits. Three of those newcomers landed immediately in the top four.

ENHYPEN's WORLD TOUR: BLOOD SAGA opened in Busan at second place overall. 2PM Concert: THE RETURN, staged in Incheon, took third. BOYNEXTDOOR's TOUR: KNOCK ON Vol.2, also in Busan, followed at fourth. A fourth pop entry, FKJ's Korean tour date in Goyang, closed out the top 10.

The pattern in those venues is worth noting. Two of the three top-charting concerts ran in Busan rather than Seoul, and the remaining two in Incheon and Goyang — all of them outside the capital proper. Large-format concert touring is the one segment of the Korean live market that routinely fills non-Seoul arenas, and this week it did so at the very top of the chart.

## Musicals Hold the Number One Slot but Lose Ground

The overall genre count was pop music 26, musical 16, play 5, Western classical music 2, and dance 1. Musicals lost share to concerts on volume, but they retained the top position and the deepest presence in the upper reaches of the ranking.

*Beethoven*, running in Seoul, held first place overall. Five more musicals filled out the rest of the top 10: *Dracula*, *Gwanghwamun Love Song*, *Billy Elliot*, *Dear Evan Hansen*, and *Geu Naldeul* — every one of them in Seoul. So while pop music won on count, the top 10 split six musicals to four concerts, and the musical half was geographically concentrated entirely in the capital.

That contrast is the real structure of the week. Two different businesses are sharing one chart. Musicals are long-run, Seoul-centered, and sell steadily across weeks; concerts are short-burst, nationally distributed, and arrive on the chart already near the top or not at all.

## Reading the Churn Numbers

Beyond the 30 new entries, eight productions climbed in rank and 11 fell. Taken together with the newcomer count, that means the chart turned over substantially: the number of shows that either debuted or slipped exceeds the number that gained ground by a wide margin.

This is the mechanical consequence of a heavy concert intake. New arena dates crowd into the upper half of a fixed 50-slot ranking, and incumbent productions — mostly running musicals and plays — are displaced downward even when their own sales are flat or improving. A falling rank in a week like this is not by itself evidence of weakening demand for a given show.

## Where the Thin Genres Sit

The smaller categories were barely present. Western classical music placed two productions on the chart and dance — covering both Western and Korean forms — placed one. Plays managed five. Combined, those three genres held eight of 50 slots, against 42 for musicals and pop music together.

That distribution is a familiar feature of box-office-ranked data rather than a measure of artistic activity. Classical concerts and dance programs typically run one or two nights in mid-sized halls, which produces revenue totals that rarely clear the threshold for a top-50 national ranking even when the houses are full. The chart tracks commercial scale, and at commercial scale the Korean stage market is a two-genre market.

## What the Two-Point Shift Actually Signals

A move from 50% to 52% is one production changing hands, and it should not be read as a structural turn on its own. What it does confirm is that concentration in Korean live performance is drifting toward concert product: the top genre's grip on the chart tightened rather than loosened, and it did so on the strength of tours that were on sale for a matter of days.

The question for the coming weeks is whether pop music holds above the halfway line once the Busan and Incheon runs cycle off. If the share falls back toward 50% without new tour announcements, this week reads as tour-calendar noise. If it holds while musicals keep the top slot, the market is settling into a durable split — capital-city long runs at the summit, national tours filling the volume beneath them.
