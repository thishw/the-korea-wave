Musical theater accounted for 44% of South Korea's 50 highest-grossing stage productions in the week of mid-July 2026, up from 32% a week earlier — a 12-percentage-point jump that made it the single largest genre on the chart, according to box office data from the Korea Performing Arts Box Office Information System (KOPIS), operated by the Korea Arts Management Service.

The shift did not come at the expense of stadium pop, which still owns the top of the ranking. It came from breadth: musicals now fill 22 of the 50 chart slots, against 21 for popular music, five for plays, one for Western classical music, and one for circus and magic.

## A Broader Chart, Not a Narrower One

The clearest structural change is the drop in concentration. The combined share of the leading genres fell from 52% to 44%, meaning revenue is spread across more titles than it was a week ago. That is the opposite of what usually happens when a handful of stadium shows land in the same week.

Twenty-nine productions entered the chart for the first time — a turnover of well over half the list. Eleven titles climbed, ten slipped. A chart that replaces this much of itself in seven days is one where short-run programming, not long-running anchors, is setting the pace.

## Stadium Shows Still Set the Ceiling

The four highest positions all went to popular music, and three of them are large-format outdoor events. Lim Young-woong's *IM HERO, THE STADIUM 2* took first place in Gyeonggi Province, followed by *BIGBANG WORLD TOUR* in Goyang, also in Gyeonggi. The Busan International Rock Festival placed third — the only top-10 entry outside the capital region other than those Gyeonggi stadium dates. N.Flying's *&CON5: into REM* in Seoul was fourth, and Red Velvet's fan concert *A Day in Red & Velvet* sixth.

Gyeonggi Province hosting the two largest events reflects a practical constraint rather than a change in audience geography: Seoul lacks stadium-scale venues available for commercial concert runs, so the biggest tours settle in the surrounding province.

## The Musical Slate Is Seoul-Concentrated and Repertoire-Heavy

Four musicals reached the top 10, all of them in Seoul: *Those Days* at fifth, *Beethoven* at seventh, *Dracula* at eighth, and *Billy Elliot* at ninth. *The Merchant of Venice*, a play, rounded out the list at tenth — also in Seoul.

The composition of that group is worth noting. *Those Days* is a Korean original built on the catalogue of the late singer Kim Kwang-seok; *Beethoven* is a domestically produced biographical work; *Dracula* and *Billy Elliot* are licensed adaptations of Western properties. A single top-10 band spans original Korean writing, jukebox format, and imported licensing — a range that reflects how established the production infrastructure around Daehangno and the Chungmuro theater district has become.

That infrastructure is also a talent pipeline that runs in both directions. Screen actors frequently trace their starts to it: Kim Mu-yeol, now known for the 2011 archery period film *War of the Arrows* and his 2012 lead in a Jung Ji-woo drama, entered the industry through a 2002 stage musical and worked roughly four years in ensemble roles before a 2005 audition for a Hakjeon Theater Company production raised his profile. He appeared in a run of *Altar Boyz* at Chungmu Art Hall's Grand Theater over ten days in May 2006. The theater feeding film and television is a long-standing pattern, and it is part of why a 44% musical share matters beyond ticket receipts.

## What a Single Week Can and Cannot Show

The 12-point swing is a weekly comparison, not a trend line. With 29 new entries, much of the movement is scheduling — musical openings clustering in the same seven days. The concentration figure falling to 44% is the more durable observation: a market where the top genres take a smaller combined share has more titles clearing the commercial threshold at once.

What would confirm a real shift is whether musical share holds above 40% once this batch of openings ages out of its premiere weeks. Nothing in a single week's ranking answers that.
